What is Teaching, Learning and Curriculum - W2

Teaching can happen without learning and learning can happen without teaching.

  • Educating: Teaching + learning

  • Curriculum: Teaching plan.

    • Aims & Objectives
    • Content
    • Evaluation
    • Teaching Strategies

  • Instructor: A person who mediates between curriculum and the students.

  • Learning is supposed to be permanent and behavior-changing

  • Teaching mostly happens with instruction.

Types of education

  • Formal Education: Follows a pre-defined plan and has grades years or semester. University is also included.

  • Non-Formal Education: For example; courses, youth program and in-service training.

    • Specialized Education: Serves special purpose and interest. For example a special course.

  • General Education: Pre school to High school. Teaches the fundamentals.

  • Informal education: A teaching type that does not have a curriculum. Like a life lesson you learn from an accident.

School is a social institution:

  1. Helps to carry society's culture into the future.
  2. Facilitates the process of social change.

Purposes of education

  1. Socialization

  2. Achievement

  3. Personal growth

  4. Social change

  • School gives information to their students through the lens of its nation. For example; history, art and science culture.

  • Education starts from birth. Maybe even before that. When people mention education we think of formal education, but we also learn from a lot of informal sources like family, peers and books.

  • A teacher has to know the subject they teach very well. Teachers need to teach the subjects essence, so the students can build on top of it.

Terms

  • Knowledge refers to a confident understanding of a subject. Potentially with the ability to use it for a specific purpose.

  • Learning is the process of acquiring knowledge, skills and attitudes.

  • Skill is an ability to perform actions. For example; academic skills, reading and computer skills.

  • Experience is an exposure to a knowledge or skill.

  • Socialization is the process by which human beings learn to adapt behavior patterns of the community.

Terms - W3

  1. Social mobility: The movement of individuals, families, households, or other categories of people within or between social strata in a society.

Purpose Of Education:

For intellectual attainment:

  1. Main point is to promote the learner's intellectual growth. Considered to be the most important responsibility of schools. Learning skill and knowledge is the main goal for the learner.

  2. Criticisms: They develop testing abilities. Not intellectual skills. It is because the tests are based on memory and not creativity.

For citizenship:

  1. States that education for citizenship is necessary to build a sense of national identity.

  2. Criticisms: They might be thought as indoctrinization. Because the curriculum focuses on the needs of the majority not the individual groups.

For vocational preparation (Mesleki Eğitim):

  1. While some say the school system has failed students that wanted to go straight into the workplace. Some say giving unnecessary classes to children might have a negative impact. Another point is that the workplace culture change too quickly for school to keep up with.

For individual development:

  1. Schools should provide learning experiences that allow learner's to maximize their individual potential.

  2. Criticisms are over-emphasis on tests, less emphasis on accommodating individual differences.

For social change:

  • Social change beyond social mobility
  • Concerns for a better society
  • Developing enlightened citizenry
  • Developing critical judgment in learners is essential for them to become good citizens of the community, fostering a healthy skepticism about traditional values and practices.

Levels of educational purposes:

National → School → Subject/Grade → Unit Plan → Lesson Plan

Foundations of Education

Changes in educational foundations does not happen because of their merits but because of the majority (often a narrow one) of educational decision makers.

Some subjects generally considered to be among the foundations of education include the following:

  1. History of education: Present school patterns can be traced back to important historic needs.

  2. Philosophy of education: Differences in educational philosophies may result in conflicts about educational decisions.

  3. Sociology of education: Through formal education, teachers are charged with transmitting elements of cultural elements for the young.

  4. Educational psychology: Educational psychologists ,who study how young people learn, don't agree on how to organize and present content in schools. Some emphasize learners' development patterns and suggest that certain learning experiences should wait until learners reach specific maturation levels. Others argue that maturation isn't a reliable predictor of what content young people can learn. Arguments like this are commonplace as psychologists clash about different subjects often.

  5. Educational curriculum and instruction: The curriculum is an overall operational plan for a school program. What goes on each day in the classroom is instruction.

  6. Legal subjects in education: Includes such issues as state and local financial support and standard for teachers.

Look for a “fit” between your philosophy of education and the educational values of the school district in which you teach.

The three main types of influential forces:

  1. Society: Shifts in influences such as the family, peer groups, social class, and the economy.

  2. Developments in knowledge: New developments in science and technology, new methods of processing and storing information, and new methods of defining or organizing fields of study.

  3. Beliefs about the nature of the learner: New theories about the learning process, may also produce changes in educational theories and purposes.

Goals

They are broad statements that denote a desired and valued competency, a theme or concern that applies to education in general. The most general goals are often called aims.

Philosophies Of Education - W4

A set of beliefs about how human beings learn and grow, and what one should learn in order to live a good life.

Traditional Philosophies:

  • Refer to complete bodies of thought.
  • Present a world-view of which education is a part.

Educational Theories:

  • Focus on education itself
    • Directly based on the practice of education in organized schools.

Perennialism

Unchanging ideas and information. This idea states that we should stick to the culture shared among humanity. Humanities values stay the same, and we should stick to the 'tried and true' method.

  • Neither education nor the truth is relative to time, place and circumstance.
  • Education, primary purpose is to bring each new generation in contact with truth.
  • Students' innate drive to know and find the truth is activated when they come in contact with the Great Books and the classic works in art, music and literature.
  • School, primary role is fostering students' intellectual and moral development.
  • Assessment is based on knowledge of the classics.

Authoritarian order, teacher decides everything.

Essentialism

Turkish education system uses this philosophie, a teacher centered idea.

School & Curriculum

  • Primary function is maintaining the basic elements of human culture by transmitting them to students as skills and subjects in a well-organized curriculum.

  • Should be practical and provide children with instruction that prepares them for life.

    • Skills of literacy, arithmetic and subjects of history, math, science, languages, and literature.

  • A subject-matter curriculum.

Teachers

  • Should be equipped with knowledge and skills to transmit skills and subjects effectively and efficiently to students.
  • Teacher-directed instruction is emphasized: guide & direct learning.

Progressivism

Education

  • Opposes essentialism and perennialism / rote memorization and authoritarian classroom management.
    • Rote memorization refers to learning through repetition and memorization without necessarily understanding the underlying concepts.
  • Children should follow their own internal timetables rather than adults' scheduling.
    • Children should not be pushed by teachers or parents to do things for which they are not ready.

Activity-based curriculum

  • Active exploration of the environment and constructing meaning of reality based on experience is the best way for children to learn.
  • Should focus on physical exercise, nature, music, crafts, field geography, story telling, dramatizations, and games.

Teacher

  • Develops knowledge and skills during teacher education.
  • Creates safe, developmentally friendly, and engaging classrooms in which children learn at their own pace.

Isolation of schools from the society Close cooperation between school and home is essential

Critical Theory

  • Critique of schools and society to uncover exploitative power relationships and bring about equity, fairness, and social justice.
  • Critical consciousness requires recognition that individual's social status is largely determined by race, ethnicity, gender, and class.
    • The dominant socioeconomic class uses its power to maintain or reproduce its favored position and to subordinate socially and economically disadvantaged classes (minorities, women, urban & rural poor, etc.)
      • Critical education enables subordinated groups to be conscious of their exploitation, resist domination and empower themselves.
  • Critical theorists want to raise consciousness of those who are forced into lesser, marginal, and subordinate positions because of race, ethnicity, class, and gender.

Official Curriculum

  • Mandated, skills and subjects purposefully selected and transmitted to students
    • Used by dominant classes to transmit particular beliefs and values
    • Transmission instead of critical thinking analysis

Hidden Curriculum

  • Approved behaviors and attitudes imposed on students through school enviroment.
    • School-based social control

Teachers (critically minded activists)

  • Can transform schools into democratic public spheres in which consciousness of the exploited is raised and empowered
    • Consciousness raising can begin with examining the conditions in their neighborhood. (Student' sharing their life stories)
    • Students can develop ways to recognize stereotyping and misrepresentation and resist indoctrination

Social roles of school

  • Prepares children to function first as young people and then as adults.
  • Transmits culture and thereby allows society to function.
  • Schools, families, peer groups, and recently, the media take a part in socializing children

Family

  • Is the early socializing agent in society and the first medium for transmitting the culture to children
  • Affect a child's orientation toward the world

School

  • Is the main institution that perpetuates culture
  • Ensures the survival and transmission of knowledge and values to future generations

Effects of major socializing institutions.

Institution: Family

  • Trends and Characteristics
    • Poverty
    • Single-parent families
    • Increase in working mothers
    • Latchkey children
    • Pressures on children
    • Overindulged children
    • Homelessness
  • Socializing Effects
    • Some trends, such as increasing numbers of working mothers and expanding options for before and after-school activities, hold positive potential, but most put children at risk for difficulty in school. Cooperative activities can reduce bullying and foster peer relationships that promote academics. Extracurricular activities tend to promote academic achievement.

Institution: Peers

  • Trends and Characteristics
    • Popularity, athletics, attractiveness more important than academics
    • Extracurricular activities with peers
    • Bullying
  • Socializing Effects
    • Cooperative activities can reduce bullying and foster peer relationships that promote academics.

Institution: School Culture

  • Trends and Characteristics
    • Active learner role carries risks
    • Hidden curriculum has diverse and frequently important impacts
    • Classroom cultures stress order
    • Accommodations, bargaining, compromise
    • Teacher overload leads to rationing attention
  • Socializing Effects
    • School often teaches students to reduce their enthusiasm and to prefer passive learning.

Institution: Television and Digital Media

  • Trends and Characteristics
    • Unclear relationship of television to achievement
    • Television may socialize aggression, undesirable attitudes
    • Internet may hold promise for active learning
  • Socializing Effects
    • Television and other media can contribute to academic achievement, but their content and use must be carefully planned, or they can become agents of negative socialization.

Equal Education Opportunity(EEO):

  • Overcoming disadvantages so that students on the average perform equally well regardless of race, ethnicity, gender, disability, social class, etc.

Necessity of EEO

  • For creation of social equity
  • For creation of a peaceful social life and reproduction of harmonious society
  • To create a community that works for the common good
  • Ensure that students acquire the knowledge, skills, and attitudes needed to participate in civic action to make society more equitable

Equal educational opportunity is protected by the constitution!!!

  • 1982 Constitution Item 42:
    • Basic education is free and served by the state
    • Educating poor is the responsibility of the state etc.

Coleman study

  • Achievement is related to student's socioeconomic background and schools with high proportions of working-class and under-class students generally received less funding than did middle-class schools
  • Moving students from mostly working-class schools to middle-class schools could improve achievement rather than simple spending money on education for disadvantaged students.

Jenks and his colleagues' study

  • Schools achievement depends substantially on students' family characteristics
  • Family background accounts for nearly half the variation in occupational status and up to 35 percent of the variation in earnings

Traditional View

  • Many working-class youth experience social mobility through school
  • Each individual who works hard has the opportunity to succeed
  • Better educated people obtain better jobs primarily because schools have made them more productive

Critical Pedagogy

  • Elite groups through schools channel disadvantaged students into worse schools, colleges, eventually jobs
  • The goal of teachers should be transformative intellectuals who work to broaden schools’ role in developing a democratic society

Multi-cultural Education

  • Refers to the various ways in which schools can take productive account of cultural backgrounds and improve opportunities for all students.
    • Focus on improving instruction for students with characteristics/background that place them at a disadvantage in traditional classrooms.
  • Multi-cultural education may help all students function in a culturally pluralistic society.

Students with disabilities

  • Main-streaming / Inclusion refers to the effort to include disabled students in a regular classroom as much as possible as it is possible and feasible
    • Inclusion approaches are not intended to eliminate special services or classes for children with exceptional needs
  • Special education usually means that a disabled student receives separate, specialized instruction for all or part of the day in a self-contained class or a resource room.

Becoming a professional teacher - W6

Problems and concerns of being a teacher

  • Shift in their roles and changing expectations

  • Classroom management skills

  • Readiness for teaching

  • Feedback from new teachers

    • They want assistance and people to talk to about their work problems. But they experience isolation, and don't have enough opportunities to share their experience.

  • Most common problems: Maintaining classroom discipline, motivating students, responding to individual differences, assessing students’ work, maintaining positive relationships with parents, organizing classroom activities, securing adequate teaching materials and supplies, dealing with the problems of individual students, lack of preparation time, conflicts with principals, difficulties with student misconduct, undesirable teaching assignments (larger class size than experienced teachers), lack of dialogue with their peers about teaching, minimal involvement in school-wide decisions

Solutions

First solution: Induction programs

  • Induction programs for beginning teachers with continued assistance throughout the first year.
    • This program should include orientation, assessment of performance, professional development workshops, mentoring/peer coaching, observation etc.

Second Solution: Classroom observations

  • Classroom observations, field experiences.

Third Solution: Practical experience

  • Practical experience, you will have a chance to instruct as a student teacher.

  • Classroom Experiences (simulations, video cases, microteaching etc.)

    • Microteaching: Students teach brief, single-concept lessons to a small group of students, you can practice your teaching skill. Feedback is given by the instructor and student peers.

  • Student teaching journals and logs can help reflection during your student-teaching experience.

    • In this journal write what you teached, your observations, your challenges and fair evaluations about yourself and your students.

  • Substitute teaching

    • Advantages:
      • Gain experience without lengthy preparation
      • Compare and contrast different schools
      • Teach and learn variety of materials
      • Networking
      • Gain confidence
      • Learn about school and district politics
      • Practice classroom management techniques
      • Flexible schedule
    • Disadvantages:
      • Pay is not good
      • Worse benefits
      • The need for adapting quickly to different school philosophies
      • Lack of continuity - each day may teach different topic

Fourth Solution: Having a mentor

  • A mentor can provide moral support, guidance & feedback to students at various stages of professional preparation and model the teacher candidate an analytical approach to solve problems in class

Continuing Professional Development

  • Reflection & self-assessment
  • Teacher workshops
  • Supervision and mentoring of student teacher
  • Conducting classroom action research: teachers conduct research in their classroom to improve instruction

A classroom-focused action research cycle

  1. Teacher identified questions, issues or problems
    • How can I more effectively motive students?
    • How do students experience the climate in my classroom?
    • What factors limit parental involvement?
    • How can teacher team become more collegial?
    • How does the use of computers affect student learning in my classroom?
  2. Observation
  3. Reflection
  4. Hypothesis generation
  5. Test hypothesis in the class
